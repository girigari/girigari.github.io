Girigari is the artistic pseudonym of Vaibhavi Kowshik — a designer, researcher, and maker working at the intersection of ecological memory, material innovation, and social impact in the Western Himalayas.

The name Girigari holds a double meaning: giri for mountain, and the act of making — of drawing, tracing, leaving a mark. It is the practice of paying attention to what the landscape already holds, and finding ways to make that visible.

पहाड़ बोलदे ने। The mountains speak.

The practice spans glass work, textile and embroidery, illustration, photography, and community workshops. It is shaped by a father who is a glass artist and a mother who is a textile designer — by hands that have always known how to make things from what is at hand.

Alongside the artistic work, Vaibhavi runs a grassroots social enterprise converting recycled glass, construction debris, and fabric waste into products for the construction industry in mountain regions — a working proof-of-concept for circular economies that function without formal infrastructure.

Over 14 years, the practice has moved between the events industry, design education, exhibition design, NGO work, and now deep field research in Kangra Valley — tracking glass waste accumulation, gathering Kurla-Kurli rain-seer oral histories from community elders, and asking what it means for a society to be truly circular.
